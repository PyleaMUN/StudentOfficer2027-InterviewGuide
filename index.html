PyleaMUN 2027

Student Officer Interview Guide

Compiled by: Alexandros Potolios, Nickolas Loukas, Fani Vasilikou, Panagiotis Domvros

Part I: Interviewer Guidelines
If the applicant gives a very good answer
  • “Thank you, that was a very well-developed response.”
  • “Interesting perspective, I appreciate the level of detail.”
  • “You explained your reasoning clearly.”
  • “Thank you, that demonstrates a good understanding of the matter.”
  • “Very good, let’s move on to the next question.”
If the applicant gives an average answer
  • “Thank you, that was helpful.”
  • “Interesting point.”
  • “I understand your reasoning.”
  • “Thank you for your response.”
If the applicant does not know the answer

If they say: “I don’t know.”

  • “That’s completely fine, let’s proceed to the next question.”
  • “No worries, we are not expecting perfection.”
  • “Thank you for your honesty, let’s move forward.”
  • “That is understandable; we’ll continue.”

If they say: “I’m not entirely sure, but I think…”

  • “Thank you for attempting an answer.”
  • “We appreciate that you tried to reason through it.”
  • “Good effort, let’s continue.”
If the applicant is very nervous
  • “Take your time; there is no need to rush.”
  • “Feel free to think for a moment before answering.”
  • “No pressure, we’re simply trying to get to know you better.”
  • “You may take a few seconds if needed.”
If the applicant gives an incorrect answer

Avoid saying “That is wrong”.

  • “Thank you for your perspective.”
  • “Interesting approach.”
  • “I understand your reasoning.”
  • “Thank you, let’s move on.”
If clarification is necessary
  • “Could you explain your thought process?”
  • “Would you like to elaborate a bit further?”
If the applicant gives a very short answer
  • “Could you expand on that a bit more?”
  • “Would you like to explain your reasoning further?”
  • “Could you give us an example?”
  • “Can you develop your answer a little more?”
Part II: Interview Question Sets
1st Section – Self Introduction
Q1. Why should we choose you as a Student Officer for PyleaMUN? Answer by connecting your skills to the values of the UN.

The candidate should connect personal skills to diplomacy, cooperation, fairness, respect, service, procedural responsibility, and the ability to support truthful and respectful dialogue. They should avoid simply listing achievements.

Follow-up: Which part of the Student Officer role do you think would challenge you most?

Q2. Who is your favourite UN Secretary-General, and why?

A strong answer should name a Secretary-General and explain the choice with a reason connected to diplomacy, peacekeeping, human rights, humanitarian work, institutional reform, or moral leadership. The candidate should avoid giving only a name without explanation.

Follow-up: Which quality from that Secretary-General would be useful for a Student Officer?

2nd Section – Rules of Procedure (ROPs) & Chairing
Q3. What is a Point of Personal Privilege, and can it interrupt a speaker?

A strong answer should explain that a Point of Personal Privilege concerns a delegate's ability to participate comfortably, such as not hearing the speaker, room conditions, or a serious personal need. It should usually not interrupt a speaker unless the issue prevents participation, especially audibility.

Follow-up: Give an example of a valid Point of Personal Privilege and an example of an invalid one.

Q4. What information must a delegate provide when proposing a Moderated Caucus?

A strong answer should mention that the delegate normally proposes a topic, total duration, individual speaking time, and the purpose or focus of the caucus. The candidate should understand that the motion must help the committee discuss a specific issue rather than simply interrupt the flow of debate.

Follow-up: How would you help a first-time delegate phrase that motion correctly?

Q5. What is the difference between substantive and procedural voting?

A strong answer should explain that procedural voting concerns the way the committee is run, such as motions and debate format, while substantive voting concerns the content of committee decisions, such as draft resolutions or amendments. The candidate should also know that abstentions are usually not allowed on procedural votes, while rules for substantive abstentions depend on delegate status and committee rules.

Follow-up: Why is this distinction important when a Chair explains voting procedure?

Q6. What is a Friendly Amendment?

A strong answer should explain that a Friendly Amendment is a proposed change to a draft resolution accepted by the sponsors or submitters according to the conference rules. It is usually incorporated more easily than an Unfriendly Amendment, but the exact process depends on the rules of procedure.

Follow-up: How would you explain the difference between a Friendly and an Unfriendly Amendment to a new delegate?

Q7. What is a Point of Parliamentary Inquiry, and can it interrupt a speaker?

A strong answer should explain that a Point of Parliamentary Inquiry is a question to the Chair about rules, procedure, or what is happening in debate. It should not normally interrupt a speaker because it is procedural rather than urgent.

Follow-up: What kind of question would be suitable for a Point of Parliamentary Inquiry?

Q8. What is voting by roll call?

A strong answer should explain that in roll-call voting, each delegation is called individually and gives its vote aloud, usually on a substantive matter. Depending on the rules, delegates may vote yes, no, abstain, or pass, and the Chair must keep the process formal, clear, and neutral.

Follow-up: When might roll-call voting be preferable to a simple show of placards?

Q9. How can a Chair protect truthful debate without taking a political side?

A strong answer should balance neutrality with responsibility. The Chair can ask for credible sources, remind delegates to use evidence, distinguish factual accuracy from political opinion, avoid public humiliation, consult a co-chair or Secretariat when necessary, and guide debate back to reliable and respectful discussion.

Follow-up: Where is the line between correcting misinformation and silencing debate?

3rd Section – Agenda
Q10. Do you think access to truthful information should be considered a human right?

A strong answer should recognise that people need reliable information to vote, stay healthy, understand conflict, claim rights, participate in society, and hold authorities accountable. A balanced candidate should also notice that defining "truth" is difficult and that truth-protection can become censorship if controlled by governments without safeguards.

Follow-up: How could the UN promote truthful information without becoming a global censor?

Q11. How can misinformation become a threat to peace and security?

Misinformation can inflame ethnic or religious hatred, justify aggression, undermine elections, spread panic during crises, weaken trust in humanitarian organisations, and make diplomacy harder. The candidate should understand that information is not a "soft" issue only; it can affect security, public health, conflict, and democratic processes.

Follow-up: Can you think of a MUN committee where this would be central?

Q12. How can the UN protect journalists when states use "fake news" laws to legally silence dissent?

A strong answer should recognise the tension between fighting harmful misinformation and protecting freedom of expression. Good answers may mention public reporting, human rights monitoring, support for journalist safety, pressure against abusive laws, independent courts, media literacy, and clear safeguards so that anti-disinformation policies are not used to punish criticism.

Follow-up: How can a government fight dangerous misinformation without criminalising legitimate journalism?

Q13. How do we balance the right to erase one's digital past with the public's right to an accurate historical record?

A strong answer should notice that privacy, rehabilitation, dignity, press freedom, transparency, and historical accountability can conflict. The best answers will avoid absolute positions and may propose proportionality, public-interest tests, age of information, seriousness of the event, whether the person is a public figure, and independent review rather than automatic deletion.

Follow-up: Should the standard be different for a private citizen and a public official?

Q14. What makes a Historical Crisis Committee different from a regular MUN committee?

A strong answer should explain that a Historical Crisis Committee is usually faster, more dynamic, and more narrative-driven than a regular committee. Delegates often represent specific characters or offices, respond to crisis updates, use directives and notes, and make decisions under time pressure rather than only drafting long resolutions.

Follow-up: What skills does a Chair need in a crisis committee that may be less central in a regular committee?

Q15. How much freedom should delegates have to change history, and where should the dais set limits?

A strong answer should balance creativity with historical plausibility. Delegates should be allowed to make meaningful choices, but the dais should prevent impossible actions, anachronistic knowledge, disrespectful handling of real suffering, or decisions that destroy the educational purpose of the simulation.

Follow-up: How would you respond if a delegate proposed an action that is entertaining but completely unrealistic?

Q16. How would you balance historical accuracy with delegate creativity?

A strong answer should explain that accuracy provides the rules of the world, while creativity lets delegates explore alternative decisions within those rules. Good answers may mention clear background guides, realistic crisis updates, historically appropriate resources, and transparent limits on what delegates can know or do.

Follow-up: Which is more important in a school MUN crisis: accuracy, engagement, or fairness? Why?

Q17. How would you explain the difference between a Draft Resolution, a Directive, and a Private Crisis Note to first-time delegates?

A strong answer should explain that a Draft Resolution is usually a formal committee text proposing broad solutions, a Directive is a shorter and faster crisis action adopted by the committee or a bloc, and a Private Crisis Note is a confidential action or request sent by an individual delegate to the crisis staff or dais. The answer should be simple, procedural, and beginner-friendly.

Follow-up: Give a one-sentence example of each in a crisis committee.

Q18. What should be the central dilemma of a Chernobyl crisis simulation?

A strong answer should focus on the conflict between public safety, scientific truth, state secrecy, evacuation, international communication, and political accountability. The candidate should recognise that the topic involves real human suffering and should be handled seriously, not as disaster entertainment.

Follow-up: How would you design the first crisis update so that delegates feel urgency without sensationalism?

Q19. What should be the central dilemma of a Cuban Missile Crisis simulation?

A strong answer should focus on escalation versus restraint: how leaders manage nuclear risk, military pressure, diplomacy, intelligence uncertainty, public messaging, and back-channel negotiation. The best answers should show that crisis design must reward careful diplomacy, not only aggressive action.

Follow-up: How would you prevent delegates from treating nuclear escalation as a game-winning shortcut?

4th Section – Crisis Situation
Q20. A delegate gives a passionate speech using a statistic that sounds false. What do you do?

The Chair should avoid publicly humiliating the delegate or taking a political side. A good response is to ask for a source, remind all delegates to use credible evidence, and, if necessary, clarify the issue neutrally during lobbying or through a calm procedural comment.

Follow-up: How would your response change if the false claim could seriously mislead the committee?

Q21. One experienced delegate dominates the debate while several first-time delegates remain silent. How do you manage this?

Strong answers should include concrete chairing strategies: balanced speaker recognition, encouraging delegates who have not spoken, using moderated caucuses with focused questions, supporting first-time delegates during lobbying, and creating a tone where confidence does not become domination.

Follow-up: How can you encourage quieter delegates without embarrassing them?

Q22. You realise during the session that you made a procedural mistake. What should you do?

A strong Student Officer should stay calm, acknowledge the mistake clearly, correct it transparently, consult a co-chair or Secretariat if needed, and continue professionally. Trying to hide the mistake is worse than correcting it.

Follow-up: How would you explain the correction to delegates?

Q23. How would you create a committee atmosphere that is serious but not intimidating?

A good Chair is professional, clear, fair, encouraging, and approachable. Strong answers may mention explaining procedure, welcoming first-time delegates, maintaining order, using respectful humour carefully, and showing that debate can be rigorous and safe.

Follow-up: What would you do in the first five minutes of the first session?

Q24. During debate on a sensitive current-affairs issue, two delegates begin using emotional or inflammatory language. How do you respond?

The Chair should protect respectful debate without shutting down difficult topics. A strong answer may include reminding delegates of diplomatic language, focusing the committee on policy and civilians rather than personal attacks, pausing debate if needed, and applying rules consistently without taking a political side.

Follow-up: What exact sentence could you say from the Chair's table to calm the room?

Q25. A delegate is visibly upset and crying. What do you do?

A strong answer should prioritise the delegate's wellbeing and dignity. The Chair should pause or quietly redirect if needed, avoid drawing unnecessary attention, ask a trusted adult or Secretariat member for support, allow the delegate to step outside, and make sure the committee continues respectfully.

Follow-up: How can you support the delegate without making them feel exposed in front of the room?

Q26. A delegate feels that they have been insulted by another committee member. How do you handle it?

A strong answer should take the concern seriously without immediately escalating the situation. The Chair should listen, clarify what happened, remind the committee of respectful conduct if appropriate, speak to the other delegate calmly, involve a senior Student Officer or teacher if necessary, and avoid turning the incident into public drama.

Follow-up: How would your response change if the insult was discriminatory or repeated?

Q27. A delegate is completely unprepared for the conference. What should a Chair do?

A strong answer should be supportive but realistic. The Chair can provide basic orientation, pair the delegate with a more experienced peer during lobbying, suggest simple ways to participate, and encourage them to focus on one or two key issues. The Chair should not shame the delegate or let lack of preparation disrupt the committee.

Follow-up: What is one quick task you could give an unprepared delegate so they can still contribute?

Q28. A delegate is being disrespectful towards the procedure and the rest of the committee. How do you respond?

A strong answer should show calm authority. The Chair should give a clear reminder of procedure and diplomatic conduct, apply rules consistently, avoid arguing with the delegate, and escalate to the Secretariat or teacher if behaviour continues. The response should protect the committee without becoming personal.

Follow-up: What would you say first before taking stronger action?

Q29. A delegate makes an insensitive joke about a sensitive global issue, such as violence, racism, or misogyny. What do you do?

A strong answer should recognise that humour can harm the tone of debate, especially around real suffering or discrimination. The Chair should respond promptly and calmly, remind delegates of respectful language, avoid turning the moment into a spectacle, and, if necessary, speak privately with the delegate or involve the Secretariat.

Follow-up: How can a Chair correct the behaviour without humiliating the delegate?

5th Section – UN Knowledge / Current Affairs
Q30. Why was the United Nations created after the Second World War?

A strong answer should connect the creation of the UN to the desire to prevent another world war, support diplomacy and collective security, encourage cooperation between states, and protect human rights after the devastation of the Second World War.

Follow-up: Which weakness of the League of Nations did the UN try to avoid?

Q31. What do you think is the biggest criticism of the UN, and how would you respond to it?

A strong answer may mention that the UN is often criticised for being slow, bureaucratic, politically divided, or unable to stop major conflicts. A balanced response should explain that the UN is not a world government and depends on Member States, while still recognising its value as a forum for diplomacy, legal norms, humanitarian coordination, peacekeeping, development work, and international pressure.

Follow-up: How would you explain the UN's limits to a delegate who says, "the UN does nothing"?

Q32. Why is 1945 such an important year in UN history?

A strong answer should know that the UN Charter was signed in San Francisco on 26 June 1945 and came into force on 24 October 1945, which is now observed as United Nations Day. The answer should connect 1945 to the end of the Second World War, the 51 founding members, and the attempt to build a stronger international system.

Follow-up: Why do we mark United Nations Day on 24 October?

Q33. What is the main purpose of the UN Charter?

A strong answer should explain that the UN Charter is the founding treaty of the United Nations. It sets out the purposes and principles of the UN, including maintaining international peace and security, developing friendly relations among nations, cooperating on international problems, and promoting human rights.

Follow-up: Why does the Charter matter in MUN debate, even when delegates are only simulating diplomacy?

Q34. What are the six principal organs of the United Nations?

A strong answer should identify the General Assembly, Security Council, Economic and Social Council, International Court of Justice, Secretariat, and Trusteeship Council. A very strong answer may add that the Trusteeship Council suspended regular operations after completing its main mandate.

Follow-up: Which of these organs is most connected to the role of a Secretary-General?

Q35. What is the difference between the General Assembly and the Security Council?

The General Assembly is the main deliberative body where all UN Member States are represented. The Security Council has 15 members and primary responsibility for international peace and security. General Assembly resolutions are usually recommendations, while Security Council decisions can be binding under the UN Charter.

Follow-up: Which body would be most relevant in a crisis involving international peace and security, and why?

Q36. The Security Council has five permanent members with veto power. Does the veto make the UN stronger or weaker? Explain both sides.

A balanced answer should explain that the veto may make the UN stronger by keeping major powers inside the system and preventing decisions they would reject. It may make the UN weaker because one permanent member can block action during serious crises, damaging credibility and frustrating smaller states.

Follow-up: Can the veto ever protect international stability?

Q37. Which states are the Permanent Members and which are the current Non-Permanent Members of the Security Council?

A strong answer should identify the five Permanent Members: China, France, the Russian Federation, the United Kingdom, and the United States. The candidate should also understand that the ten Non-Permanent Members are elected for two-year terms and change regularly. Interviewers should verify the current list before interviews; as of 2026, the Non-Permanent Members are Bahrain, Colombia, Democratic Republic of the Congo, Denmark, Greece, Latvia, Liberia, Pakistan, Panama, and Somalia.

Follow-up: Why do the Non-Permanent Members matter if they do not have veto power?

Q38. What is the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR), and when was it adopted?

A strong answer should explain that the UDHR is a landmark international human rights document adopted by the UN General Assembly on 10 December 1948. It is not a treaty in itself, but it strongly influenced later human rights law, constitutions, and international standards.

Follow-up: Why is the UDHR still important if it is not a treaty?

Q39. What is the difference between a recommendation and a binding decision? Why does this matter in MUN?

A recommendation expresses what a body believes should happen, while a binding decision creates an obligation under the relevant rules or law. This matters in MUN because different committees have different powers: General Assembly committees usually recommend action, while the Security Council can make binding decisions on peace and security.

Follow-up: How should this difference affect the wording of a draft resolution?

Q40. Should the UN Security Council be reformed?

A strong answer should present both sides. Reform arguments may mention that the Council reflects the power structure of 1945 more than today's world, and that Africa, Latin America, and other regions may need stronger representation. Cautionary arguments may mention that reform is politically difficult and could make decision-making slower.

Follow-up: Which reform would be most realistic, not just most desirable?

Q41. What role can the United Nations play during international conflicts?

The candidate should understand that the UN is not a world government. Strong answers may mention diplomacy, Security Council and General Assembly resolutions, humanitarian aid, peacekeeping where authorised, investigations of international law violations, negotiation forums, and the limits caused by political disagreement or vetoes.

Follow-up: Can you give one example of a conflict where Security Council disagreement limits UN action?

Q42. Why is the conflict in Gaza important for international relations?

The answer should use objective language and mention regional stability, humanitarian access, protection of civilians, international humanitarian law, hostage and detainee issues, mediation, and prospects for a political settlement. It should avoid inflammatory or one-sided phrasing.

Follow-up: How can a Chair keep debate respectful when the topic is emotionally sensitive?

Q43. How does the war in Ukraine connect to core UN principles?

A strong answer should mention sovereignty, territorial integrity, the prohibition on the use of force, collective security, sanctions, accountability, and the limits of the Security Council when a permanent member is directly involved in a conflict.

Follow-up: Why are General Assembly resolutions still important even when they are not binding?

Q44. What are sanctions, and when might the UN use them?

Sanctions are non-military measures such as travel bans, asset freezes, arms embargoes, or trade restrictions. Strong answers should explain that sanctions may influence behaviour, support peace processes, counter terrorism, prevent proliferation, or respond to threats to peace, while also recognising risks to civilians if sanctions are not carefully targeted.

Follow-up: Are targeted sanctions fairer than broad economic sanctions?

Q45. Why is artificial intelligence governance an international issue rather than only a national one?

The answer should connect AI with human rights, bias, transparency, data, elections, education, labour markets, surveillance, security, digital divides, and possible military uses. The candidate should avoid giving a purely technical answer.

Follow-up: Which UN body or international framework could be relevant to AI governance?

Q46. What are your views on femicides as a human rights and rule-of-law issue?

A strong answer should recognise femicides as connected to wider patterns of gender-based violence, discrimination, failures of protection, access to justice, equality before the law, and the right to life and security. Good answers may mention prevention, reliable data collection, education, police and judicial training, protection mechanisms, victim and survivor support, and accountability. The candidate should avoid victim-blaming or sensational language.

Follow-up: What could a UN committee recommend without replacing the role of national courts?

Q47. Why do some humanitarian crises become "forgotten crises"?

Strong answers may mention lack of media access, limited strategic interest for major powers, competition with more visible conflicts, donor fatigue, and the difficulty of keeping public attention on long-running emergencies. The candidate should show empathy as well as structural analysis.

Follow-up: How could a MUN committee bring attention to a forgotten crisis?

Q48. What does "multipolarity" mean, and why does it matter for the UN?

Multipolarity means that power is distributed among several influential states or blocs rather than dominated by one or two. A strong answer may connect this to the Global South, BRICS, Security Council reform, new partnerships, rivalry between powers, and the difficulty of building consensus.

Follow-up: Does multipolarity strengthen or weaken multilateralism?

6th Section – Closure

Section script

This is the final section of the interview and will be carried out by the interviewer responsible for the first section. The interviewer will proceed with two questions: one intended to reveal the applicant’s personality traits and one concerning their motivation for applying.

“Moving back to me, this will be the last—and perhaps more enjoyable—part of the interview.”

I would like you to briefly state your reason for applying and explain why we should choose you over another applicant.

  • At the end of the conference, what do you want delegates to remember from your committee?
  • What do you consider your greatest strength and your greatest weakness as a potential Chair?
  • Lastly, if you were an animal, which animal would you be and why?
“Do you have any questions for us? This concludes the interview. Thank you for your time and for your application. You will be notified of the results through email by the Secretariat within the next few days.”
Historical Crisis Committees: Further Specialized Interview Questions & Expected Answers
1. Core Historical Crisis Chairing Skills
1.1 General Historical Crisis Committee Mindset
What makes a Historical Crisis Committee different from a regular MUN committee?
A strong answer should mention that a Historical Crisis Committee is more dynamic, role-based, and time-sensitive than a regular committee. Delegates do not only debate long-term policy; they also react to sudden developments, submit directives, use private crisis notes, and make choices under pressure. The Chair must manage both historical atmosphere and crisis consequences.
How much freedom should delegates have to change history, and where should the dais set limits?
Delegates should have meaningful freedom within historically plausible boundaries. They may alter outcomes, alliances, strategies, and decisions, but they should not ignore basic historical realities, technology, geography, political structures, or the powers of their assigned role. The dais should reward creative plausibility, not fantasy.
How would you balance historical accuracy with delegate creativity?
The guide should provide a historically accurate starting point, clear role powers, and realistic constraints. During the committee, delegates should be free to explore alternative outcomes, but every action should have believable causes and consequences. The Chair should guide, not force, history.
What should delegates learn from a Historical Crisis Committee beyond simply “winning” the simulation?
They should learn historical thinking, decision-making under pressure, the consequences of misinformation, ethical leadership, compromise, and the difficulty of acting with incomplete knowledge. The goal is not just victory but understanding complexity.
1.2 Chairing, Procedure, and Committee Control
How would you open the first session in a way that immediately establishes atmosphere and authority?
A strong candidate should propose a short, dramatic opening statement that sets the historical scene, reminds delegates of the stakes, and explains the committee’s immediate mission. The Chair should sound confident, organised, and historically immersive without wasting time.
How would you explain the difference between a Draft Resolution, a Directive, and a Private Crisis Note to first-time delegates?
A Draft Resolution is a formal long-term document. A Directive is a short, immediate action plan used during a crisis. A Private Crisis Note is secret communication with the crisis staff, usually using a delegate’s specific role, asset, or portfolio power.
Explain the difference between a Directive and a Draft Resolution.
A Draft Resolution is formal, long-term, and usually includes preambulatory and operative clauses. A Directive is short, immediate, and action-oriented, used to respond quickly to crisis developments.
What would you do if the committee becomes chaotic during a crisis update?
The Chair should pause debate, restate the crisis clearly, set a time limit, define the expected output, and guide delegates into blocs or working groups. The Chair should not shout or improvise wildly; they should restore structure.
How would you keep quieter or less experienced delegates involved in a fast-moving crisis?
The Chair can call on them during moderated caucus, assign them useful procedural roles, encourage bloc leaders to include them, ask targeted questions, and design crisis updates that require input from different types of delegates rather than only the loudest ones.
What would you do if delegates are not producing any meaningful documents or decisions?
The Chair should clarify the task, give a document template, set a deadline, and possibly introduce a crisis update that forces decision-making. The goal is to move from general debate to concrete written action.
2. Committee 1: The Chernobyl Disaster / The Cuban Missile Crisis
2.1 The Chernobyl Disaster: The Cost of Lies
What should be the central dilemma of a Chernobyl crisis simulation?
The central dilemma is whether authorities prioritise public safety and scientific truth or political reputation and secrecy. Delegates must decide how much to reveal, when to evacuate, how to contain radiation, and how to manage international scrutiny.
How would you represent the conflict between containing radiation and containing information?
Radiation should spread according to physical reality, while information spreads through reports, rumours, foreign detection, media pressure, and internal bureaucracy. Attempts to hide the disaster may buy political time but worsen health, trust, and diplomatic consequences.
How would you handle a delegate who wants to hide the disaster completely from the public?
A strong Chair should allow the attempt if it fits the role, but impose realistic consequences: radiation readings abroad, rumours from workers, worsening casualties, delayed evacuation, international suspicion, and loss of trust. Hiding the truth should be playable, but not consequence-free.
How would you connect Chernobyl to the idea of “epistemic security”?
Epistemic security means protecting the conditions under which people can know what is true. Chernobyl shows that suppressing facts can become a security threat because bad information leads to bad decisions, public harm, and institutional collapse.
2.2 The Cuban Missile Crisis: SIGINT and Brinkmanship

Key concept

Information brinkmanship and the management of signal intelligence: a crisis where leaders must make dangerous decisions using incomplete intelligence, while sending and interpreting signals carefully enough to avoid accidental nuclear war.

What should be the central dilemma of a Cuban Missile Crisis simulation?
The central dilemma is how to prevent nuclear war while acting on incomplete intelligence and under political pressure. Delegates must decide when to signal strength, when to compromise, and how to avoid miscalculation.
How would you prevent the committee from becoming a simple USA versus USSR debate?
Cuba, allies, military factions, diplomats, intelligence agencies, and domestic political pressures should all matter. The committee should not only ask “Who wins?” but “How do leaders interpret dangerous signals under pressure?”
3. Committee 2: Special Tribunal on Intellectual & Political Subversion
3.1 Tribunal Design
How is a historical tribunal different from a normal crisis committee?
A tribunal focuses on argument, evidence, testimony, judgment, and legal or moral reasoning. It can still include crisis elements, but the main structure is not only policy debate; it is a contested case.
How would you stop the trial from becoming a scripted theatre performance?
Delegates should have flexible roles, uncertain evidence, strategic choices, and opportunities to challenge testimony. The dais should avoid giving a fixed ending. The trial should be structured, but the result should depend on delegate performance.
What should the final output of the committee be: a verdict, legal opinion, decree, political settlement, or public statement?
A strong answer should justify the chosen output. For a tribunal, a verdict or legal opinion is essential, but it may be accompanied by a decree, public statement, or settlement explaining the political consequences of the judgment.
3.2 Aspasia of Miletus: Character Assassination as a Political Weapon
What should be the central dilemma of the Aspasia trial?
The central dilemma is whether the trial is a genuine moral/legal case or a political attack using morality and reputation as weapons. Delegates must examine personal freedom, public morality, gender, and power in democratic Athens.
How would you show that the case is not only about Aspasia personally, but also about political attacks on Pericles and his circle?
The guide and crisis updates should show Aspasia’s connection to elite politics, intellectual life, and Pericles’ enemies. The prosecution may use her reputation to weaken Pericles indirectly, while the defence should expose the political motives behind the accusations.
How would you represent character assassination and gendered disinformation in a historically plausible way?
The committee should use rumours, speeches, social reputation, comic exaggeration, and accusations of impiety or improper influence. However, it should avoid modern language that feels anachronistic unless carefully explained.
3.3 Hypatia of Alexandria: Scientific Truth vs. Religious Populism
What should be the central dilemma of the Hypatia defence?
The central dilemma is whether a public intellectual can survive in a politically unstable society where knowledge, religion, civic authority, and mob pressure are intertwined. The case should ask whether fear can turn learning into “subversion.”
How would you frame the conflict between scientific truth and religious populism without making the committee anti-religious?
The conflict should not be presented as “science good, religion bad.” Instead, it should focus on political manipulation, public fear, factional rivalry, and the misuse of religious language for power. Religious actors should be diverse, not one-dimensional.
4. Scenario-Based Interview Questions
4.1 Crisis Staff Judgement
A delegate submits a historically impossible action but explains it very persuasively. What do you do?
The Chair should praise the creativity but reject or modify the action to fit historical plausibility. The candidate should not allow impossible technology, impossible authority, or unrealistic power simply because the delegate argues well.
Strong delegates are dominating private notes, while weaker delegates are being ignored. How do you rebalance the committee?
The dais can slow down note processing, create role-specific opportunities, ask quieter delegates for input, design updates that require overlooked portfolios, and encourage public coalition-building. The Chair should not punish strong delegates, but should widen participation.
A delegate makes an insensitive joke about radiation sickness, nuclear war, misogyny, or religious violence. What do you do?
The Chair should intervene calmly and immediately. They should remind the room of the seriousness of the topic, set expectations for respectful debate, and, if necessary, speak privately to the delegate or involve senior staff. The issue should not be ignored.
5. Practical Tasks for Candidates
5.1 Core Procedure Check
In two minutes, explain the difference between a Directive and a Draft Resolution.
A Draft Resolution is formal, long-term, and usually includes preambulatory and operative clauses. A Directive is short, immediate, and action-oriented, used to respond quickly to crisis developments.
6. Reflection and Suitability Questions
6.1 Candidate Suitability
Why do you want to serve as a Student Officer in a Historical Crisis Committee?
A strong answer should show genuine interest in history, crisis debate, leadership, and helping delegates learn. It should not focus only on status or title.
Which of the two 2027 Historical Crisis Committees are you more suited to chair, and why?
The candidate should connect their strengths to the committee’s demands. Committee 1 requires fast crisis management, intelligence handling, and information control. Committee 2 requires legal structure, sensitivity, evidence management, and strong moderation.
Which topic would be most difficult for you personally, and why?
A strong candidate should answer honestly and show self-awareness. They should identify a challenge and explain how they would prepare for it.
What is your greatest strength and your greatest weakness as a potential Chair?

At the end of the conference, what should delegates remember from your committee?
