Student Officer Interview Guide

Part I: Interviewer Guidelines
If the applicant gives a very good answer
  • “Thank you, that was a very well-developed response.”
  • “Interesting perspective, I appreciate the level of detail.”
  • “You explained your reasoning clearly.”
  • “Thank you, that demonstrates a good understanding of the matter.”
  • “Very good, let’s move on to the next question.”
If the applicant gives an average answer
  • “Thank you, that was helpful.”
  • “Interesting point.”
  • “I understand your reasoning.”
  • “Thank you for your response.”
If the applicant does not know the answer

If they say: “I don’t know.”

  • “That’s completely fine, let’s proceed to the next question.”
  • “No worries, we are not expecting perfection.”
  • “Thank you for your honesty, let’s move forward.”
  • “That is understandable; we’ll continue.”

If they say: “I’m not entirely sure, but I think…”

  • “Thank you for attempting an answer.”
  • “We appreciate that you tried to reason through it.”
  • “Good effort, let’s continue.”
If the applicant is very nervous
  • “Take your time; there is no need to rush.”
  • “Feel free to think for a moment before answering.”
  • “No pressure, we’re simply trying to get to know you better.”
  • “You may take a few seconds if needed.”
If the applicant gives an incorrect answer

Avoid saying “That is wrong”.

  • “Thank you for your perspective.”
  • “Interesting approach.”
  • “I understand your reasoning.”
  • “Thank you, let’s move on.”
If clarification is necessary
  • “Could you explain your thought process?”
  • “Would you like to elaborate a bit further?”
If the applicant gives a very short answer
  • “Could you expand on that a bit more?”
  • “Would you like to explain your reasoning further?”
  • “Could you give us an example?”
  • “Can you develop your answer a little more?”

2nd Section – Rules of Procedure (ROPs) & Chairing

Section script

In this section, the applicant will be asked questions concerning the PyleaMUN Rules of Procedure. The interviewer responsible for this section will proceed with questions regarding the applicant’s preferred committees, as well as one question concerning a specialized agency.

“I will now proceed with the next part of the interview, where I would like to focus on the Rules of Procedure and committee management. What is the committee in which you long for this role?”
Group A
What is a Point of Personal Privilege and can it interrupt a speaker?
Used for personal discomfort, e.g., cannot hear. It can ONLY interrupt a speaker if it concerns audibility.
What information must a delegate provide when proposing a Moderated Caucus?
Total duration, individual speaking time, and the topic.
What is the difference between substantive and procedural voting?
Procedural is for motions/structure and everyone must vote. Substantive is for draft resolutions/amendments and delegates can abstain.
What is a Friendly Amendment?
Group B
What is the difference between a Point of Order and a Point of Parliamentary Inquiry?
Point of Order is to point out a mistake in the ROPs made by the Chair or a delegate. Parliamentary Inquiry is to ask the Chair a question regarding the ROPs.
What is the difference between Adjournment of the Meeting and Adjournment of the Debate?
Adjournment of the Meeting ends the session for the day/conference. Adjournment of the Debate pauses the current topic to move to another.
What is an Unfriendly Amendment?
Unfriendly is not agreed upon by all main submitters and requires a committee vote.
What voting options are available to delegates?
Yes, No, Abstain; sometimes Yes with Rights/No with Rights.
Group C
What is a Point of Parliamentary Inquiry and can it interrupt a speaker?
It is to ask the Chair a question regarding the ROPs and cannot interrupt the speaker.
What information is required for an Unmoderated Caucus motion?
Duration.
What is voting by roll call?
It is typically used during substantive voting. The Chair calls the roll in alphabetical order, and each delegate must state their vote aloud for the official record.
What is the difference between Adjournment of the Meeting and Adjournment of the Debate?
Adjournment of the Meeting ends the session for the day/conference. Adjournment of the Debate pauses the current topic to move to another.
Security Council
Can a P5 member veto a procedural vote? Does an abstention count as a veto?
No to both. Vetoes only apply to substantive votes, and an abstention allows the resolution to pass.
Which states are the Permanent Members and which are the current Non-Permanent Members of the Security Council?
Permanent Members: China, France, Russian Federation, United Kingdom, United States. Current Non-Permanent Members: Denmark, Greece, Pakistan, Panama, Somalia, Bahrain, Colombia, Democratic Republic of the Congo, Latvia, Liberia.
What is the veto power?
The veto power is the exclusive right of the council's five permanent members to unilaterally block any substantive resolution or decision, regardless of how much global support the measure has.
NATO
How are decisions traditionally made in NATO?
Consensus.
What should happen if one member objects to a proposal in NATO?
The decision fails.
What does consensus mean in a NATO simulation?
All member states must agree on a decision before it can pass. There is no majority voting.

Committee Question Bank
Security Council 1 (UNSC)

Topic 1

If a highly realistic deep fake video of a head of state declaring war were released, should the international community treat the creator as a conventional aggressor/offender?

Should weigh the “epistemic threat” and intent to incite kinetic conflict vs. the difficulty of attribution and non-physical nature of the threat.

Topic 2

At what point does delegating military decisions to AI become incompatible with international humanitarian law?

When there is a loss of “meaningful human control” over lethal autonomous systems, risking “flash wars”.

Security Council 2 (UNSC)

Topic 1

Should states be held legally responsible for cyber operations conducted by private contractors acting on their behalf?

Delegates must address the “plausible deniability” these “digital mercenaries” provide and debate legal accountability.

Topic 2

What criteria should determine whether a cyberattack constitutes an “armed attack” under the UN Charter?

Look at the severity of the target, such as national power grids or banking systems, and the resulting physical/economic damage.

Committee on Digital Affairs & Sovereignty

Topic 1

Can meaningful cyber-sovereignty exist without fragmenting the internet into isolated national networks?

Balance binding rules for Tech Giants, algorithmic transparency, data ownership, and open digital infrastructure.

Topic 2

Should governments have the authority to regulate data transmitted through privately owned satellite internet systems operating over their territory?

Balance global connectivity against state information control, national firewalls, and orbital safety.

ECOSOC

Topic 1

How can governments protect workers from AI-driven displacement without stifling technological innovation?

Manage the shift in the “Truth Economy”, protect IP from being scraped without consent, and manage labour market shifts.

Topic 2

Can decentralized financial systems (DeFi) realistically be regulated without undermining the very principle of decentralization?

Address shadow banking and threats to developing nations while acknowledging that the assets are designed to be outside state control.

UNESCO

Topic 1

Who should have the authority to determine what constitutes a historically accurate representation in virtual environments?

Create frameworks to ensure AI does not erase historical truths of marginalized communities or lead to “digital revisionism”.

Topic 2

Should indigenous communities have the right to prohibit AI systems from using their cultural knowledge for training purposes?

Yes, frameworks must be created to stop the harvesting of oral histories without consent and to protect endangered languages.

UN Environment Programme (UNEP)

Topic 1

Should corporations face international legal penalties for deliberately misleading the public about their environmental impact?

Address “greenwashing” and ensure global climate goals are based on facts, not manipulated marketing.

Topic 2

How can the international community regulate the immense energy consumption required for AI development and crypto-mining?

Formulate international standards for sustainable digital infrastructure to offset the massive carbon footprint of data centers.

World Health Organisation (WHO)

Topic 1

Where should the balance lie between protecting youth mental health and preserving freedom of expression on social media?

Formulate guidelines to combat algorithmic diet trends and body dysmorphia without overly censoring user content.

Topic 2

How can the WHO effectively combat “medical populism” when public trust in evidence-based science is eroding?

Create a unified response to health-related “fake news” that impacts global health security and pandemic preparedness.

Human Rights Council (HRC)

Topic 1

How can the UN protect journalists when states use “fake news” laws to legally silence dissent?

Address digital authoritarianism, surveillance, and state-mandated internet shutdowns to protect truth-tellers.

Topic 2

How do we balance the right to erase one’s digital past with the public’s right to an accurate historical record?

Navigate the legal tension between individual privacy and the transparency needed for public accountability.

UN WOMEN

Topic 1

Should platforms be held directly liable for AI-generated non-consensual intimate content (deep fakes) shared on their services?

Address how digital tools are weaponized to silence women and the need to combat online misogyny.

Topic 2

Can AI-driven hiring systems ever be truly free from gender bias, and if not, what safeguards should exist?

Create ethical AI guidelines to ensure automated systems do not reinforce financial or workforce discrimination.

SOCHUM

Topic 1

Could digital citizenship become a viable substitute for traditional nationality for stateless persons, or would it create new forms of exclusion?

Ensure biometric ID serves as a tool for inclusion, not surveillance, for those lacking traditional state documents.

Topic 2

How can the UN prevent “digital colonialism” regarding the mass-scraping of indigenous cultural data?

Formulate rights-based frameworks so digital platforms do not perpetuate colonial power dynamics by taking data without consent.

UNODC

Topic 1

How can governments combat dark-web criminal markets for synthetic narcotics without compromising citizens’ digital privacy rights?

Develop international strategies to track “cyber-syndicates” while maintaining human rights online.

Topic 2

Should governments ever negotiate or pay ransomware groups when critical public infrastructure is at risk?

Develop shared legal protocols to protect public hospitals and utilities from transnational extortion.

NATO

Topic 1

How should the Alliance respond to non-kinetic “cognitive warfare” aimed at eroding trust in democratic institutions?

Formulate strategies against social media bots and information warfare that target the mind as a domain of war.

Topic 2

What measures can be taken to physically secure transatlantic data cables from state-sponsored sabotage?

Ensure the continuity of intelligence sharing by protecting the undersea physical backbone of the internet.

4th Section – Crisis Situation

Section script

In this section, the applicant will be expected to provide effective ideas on how they would manage potential situations they may encounter as a Student Officer during PyleaMUN. The interviewer responsible for this section will present two scenarios from the list below:

“Now moving on to the next part of the interview, as you may know, a Student Officer is expected to be capable of handling situations that may arise during committee sessions.”

With that being said, how would you deal with:

A delegate being visibly upset and crying.
Immediately pause, gently ask the delegate to step outside with a member of the admin staff or co-chair, ensure their privacy, comfort them, and notify the Secretariat/Advisors. Do not draw committee attention to them.
A delegate feeling that they have been insulted by another committee member.
Remind the committee of diplomatic courtesy. If it was severe, issue a warning to the offending delegate. Speak to both privately during a break to mediate.
A delegate being completely unprepared for the conference.
Do not embarrass them. During unmoderated caucuses, guide them towards delegates with similar policies, offer them a template for clauses, and gently encourage them to read their study guide.
A delegate being disrespectful towards the procedure and the rest of the committee.
Issue a firm verbal warning. If the behavior continues, revoke their speaking rights temporarily. If it escalates, involve the Secretariat.
A delegate makes an insensitive joke regarding a sensitive global issue, e.g., violence, racism, misogyny.
Intervene calmly and immediately. Remind the room of the seriousness of the topic and set expectations for respectful debate. Speak privately to the delegate and involve senior staff if necessary.

Historical Crisis Committees: Interview Questions & Expected Answers
1. Core Historical Crisis Chairing Skills
1.1 General Historical Crisis Committee Mindset
What makes a Historical Crisis Committee different from a regular MUN committee?
A strong answer should mention that a Historical Crisis Committee is more dynamic, role-based, and time-sensitive than a regular committee. Delegates do not only debate long-term policy; they also react to sudden developments, submit directives, use private crisis notes, and make choices under pressure. The Chair must manage both historical atmosphere and crisis consequences.
How much freedom should delegates have to change history, and where should the dais set limits?
Delegates should have meaningful freedom within historically plausible boundaries. They may alter outcomes, alliances, strategies, and decisions, but they should not ignore basic historical realities, technology, geography, political structures, or the powers of their assigned role. The dais should reward creative plausibility, not fantasy.
How would you balance historical accuracy with delegate creativity?
The guide should provide a historically accurate starting point, clear role powers, and realistic constraints. During the committee, delegates should be free to explore alternative outcomes, but every action should have believable causes and consequences. The Chair should guide, not force, history.
What should delegates learn from a Historical Crisis Committee beyond simply “winning” the simulation?
They should learn historical thinking, decision-making under pressure, the consequences of misinformation, ethical leadership, compromise, and the difficulty of acting with incomplete knowledge. The goal is not just victory but understanding complexity.
1.2 Chairing, Procedure, and Committee Control
How would you open the first session in a way that immediately establishes atmosphere and authority?
A strong candidate should propose a short, dramatic opening statement that sets the historical scene, reminds delegates of the stakes, and explains the committee’s immediate mission. The Chair should sound confident, organised, and historically immersive without wasting time.
How would you explain the difference between a Draft Resolution, a Directive, and a Private Crisis Note to first-time delegates?
A Draft Resolution is a formal long-term document. A Directive is a short, immediate action plan used during a crisis. A Private Crisis Note is secret communication with the crisis staff, usually using a delegate’s specific role, asset, or portfolio power.
Explain the difference between a Directive and a Draft Resolution.
A Draft Resolution is formal, long-term, and usually includes preambulatory and operative clauses. A Directive is short, immediate, and action-oriented, used to respond quickly to crisis developments.
What would you do if the committee becomes chaotic during a crisis update?
The Chair should pause debate, restate the crisis clearly, set a time limit, define the expected output, and guide delegates into blocs or working groups. The Chair should not shout or improvise wildly; they should restore structure.
How would you keep quieter or less experienced delegates involved in a fast-moving crisis?
The Chair can call on them during moderated caucus, assign them useful procedural roles, encourage bloc leaders to include them, ask targeted questions, and design crisis updates that require input from different types of delegates rather than only the loudest ones.
What would you do if delegates are not producing any meaningful documents or decisions?
The Chair should clarify the task, give a document template, set a deadline, and possibly introduce a crisis update that forces decision-making. The goal is to move from general debate to concrete written action.
2. Committee 1: The Chernobyl Disaster / The Cuban Missile Crisis
2.1 The Chernobyl Disaster: The Cost of Lies
What should be the central dilemma of a Chernobyl crisis simulation?
The central dilemma is whether authorities prioritise public safety and scientific truth or political reputation and secrecy. Delegates must decide how much to reveal, when to evacuate, how to contain radiation, and how to manage international scrutiny.
How would you represent the conflict between containing radiation and containing information?
Radiation should spread according to physical reality, while information spreads through reports, rumours, foreign detection, media pressure, and internal bureaucracy. Attempts to hide the disaster may buy political time but worsen health, trust, and diplomatic consequences.
How would you handle a delegate who wants to hide the disaster completely from the public?
A strong Chair should allow the attempt if it fits the role, but impose realistic consequences: radiation readings abroad, rumours from workers, worsening casualties, delayed evacuation, international suspicion, and loss of trust. Hiding the truth should be playable, but not consequence-free.
How would you connect Chernobyl to the idea of “epistemic security”?
Epistemic security means protecting the conditions under which people can know what is true. Chernobyl shows that suppressing facts can become a security threat because bad information leads to bad decisions, public harm, and institutional collapse.
2.2 The Cuban Missile Crisis: SIGINT and Brinkmanship

Key concept

Information brinkmanship and the management of signal intelligence: a crisis where leaders must make dangerous decisions using incomplete intelligence, while sending and interpreting signals carefully enough to avoid accidental nuclear war.

What should be the central dilemma of a Cuban Missile Crisis simulation?
The central dilemma is how to prevent nuclear war while acting on incomplete intelligence and under political pressure. Delegates must decide when to signal strength, when to compromise, and how to avoid miscalculation.
How would you prevent the committee from becoming a simple USA versus USSR debate?
Cuba, allies, military factions, diplomats, intelligence agencies, and domestic political pressures should all matter. The committee should not only ask “Who wins?” but “How do leaders interpret dangerous signals under pressure?”
3. Committee 2: Special Tribunal on Intellectual & Political Subversion
3.1 Tribunal Design
How is a historical tribunal different from a normal crisis committee?
A tribunal focuses on argument, evidence, testimony, judgment, and legal or moral reasoning. It can still include crisis elements, but the main structure is not only policy debate; it is a contested case.
How would you stop the trial from becoming a scripted theatre performance?
Delegates should have flexible roles, uncertain evidence, strategic choices, and opportunities to challenge testimony. The dais should avoid giving a fixed ending. The trial should be structured, but the result should depend on delegate performance.
What should the final output of the committee be: a verdict, legal opinion, decree, political settlement, or public statement?
A strong answer should justify the chosen output. For a tribunal, a verdict or legal opinion is essential, but it may be accompanied by a decree, public statement, or settlement explaining the political consequences of the judgment.
3.2 Aspasia of Miletus: Character Assassination as a Political Weapon
What should be the central dilemma of the Aspasia trial?
The central dilemma is whether the trial is a genuine moral/legal case or a political attack using morality and reputation as weapons. Delegates must examine personal freedom, public morality, gender, and power in democratic Athens.
How would you show that the case is not only about Aspasia personally, but also about political attacks on Pericles and his circle?
The guide and crisis updates should show Aspasia’s connection to elite politics, intellectual life, and Pericles’ enemies. The prosecution may use her reputation to weaken Pericles indirectly, while the defence should expose the political motives behind the accusations.
How would you represent character assassination and gendered disinformation in a historically plausible way?
The committee should use rumours, speeches, social reputation, comic exaggeration, and accusations of impiety or improper influence. However, it should avoid modern language that feels anachronistic unless carefully explained.
3.3 Hypatia of Alexandria: Scientific Truth vs. Religious Populism
What should be the central dilemma of the Hypatia defence?
The central dilemma is whether a public intellectual can survive in a politically unstable society where knowledge, religion, civic authority, and mob pressure are intertwined. The case should ask whether fear can turn learning into “subversion.”
How would you frame the conflict between scientific truth and religious populism without making the committee anti-religious?
The conflict should not be presented as “science good, religion bad.” Instead, it should focus on political manipulation, public fear, factional rivalry, and the misuse of religious language for power. Religious actors should be diverse, not one-dimensional.
4. Scenario-Based Interview Questions
4.1 Crisis Staff Judgement
A delegate submits a historically impossible action but explains it very persuasively. What do you do?
The Chair should praise the creativity but reject or modify the action to fit historical plausibility. The candidate should not allow impossible technology, impossible authority, or unrealistic power simply because the delegate argues well.
Strong delegates are dominating private notes, while weaker delegates are being ignored. How do you rebalance the committee?
The dais can slow down note processing, create role-specific opportunities, ask quieter delegates for input, design updates that require overlooked portfolios, and encourage public coalition-building. The Chair should not punish strong delegates, but should widen participation.
A delegate makes an insensitive joke about radiation sickness, nuclear war, misogyny, or religious violence. What do you do?
The Chair should intervene calmly and immediately. They should remind the room of the seriousness of the topic, set expectations for respectful debate, and, if necessary, speak privately to the delegate or involve senior staff. The issue should not be ignored.
5. Practical Tasks for Candidates
5.1 Core Procedure Check
In two minutes, explain the difference between a Directive and a Draft Resolution.
A Draft Resolution is formal, long-term, and usually includes preambulatory and operative clauses. A Directive is short, immediate, and action-oriented, used to respond quickly to crisis developments.
6. Reflection and Suitability Questions
6.1 Candidate Suitability
Why do you want to serve as a Student Officer in a Historical Crisis Committee?
A strong answer should show genuine interest in history, crisis debate, leadership, and helping delegates learn. It should not focus only on status or title.
Which of the two 2027 Historical Crisis Committees are you more suited to chair, and why?
The candidate should connect their strengths to the committee’s demands. Committee 1 requires fast crisis management, intelligence handling, and information control. Committee 2 requires legal structure, sensitivity, evidence management, and strong moderation.
Which topic would be most difficult for you personally, and why?
A strong candidate should answer honestly and show self-awareness. They should identify a challenge and explain how they would prepare for it.
What is your greatest strength and your greatest weakness as a potential Chair?
The answer should be specific. Good strengths include organisation, calmness, historical knowledge, fairness, or public speaking. Good weaknesses should be realistic but manageable, followed by a plan for improvement.
At the end of the conference, what should delegates remember from your committee?
They should remember that history is shaped by difficult choices, limited information, power, fear, and responsibility. They should feel that their decisions mattered and that the committee was both intellectually serious and memorable.
